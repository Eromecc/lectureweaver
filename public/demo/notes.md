# Evidence-Based Study Strategies

These notes collect the study methods I plan to use before exams.

## Retrieval Practice

Retrieval practice means closing the material and recalling the answer before checking. Low-stakes quizzes, blank-page recall, and explaining a process from memory reveal gaps and strengthen access. My loop is: learn, retrieve, check, then restudy what I missed.

## Spaced Practice

Spacing distributes practice across separate sessions instead of cramming it into one sitting. Some forgetting makes the next retrieval effortful, so I should revisit a topic over increasing intervals instead of repeating it ten times in one night.

Practice Schedule
-----------------

I will schedule short, cumulative reviews after about one day, three days, seven days, and fourteen days. If recall fails, I will shorten the next interval; if recall is easy, I will lengthen it. Older topics should stay in the weekly review queue.

## Desirable Difficulties

A desirable difficulty feels harder during practice but improves later retention or transfer. It must still be achievable and connected to the learning goal. Generation before explanation, varied examples, and spaced retrieval are useful; confusion for its own sake is not.

### Interleaving

Interleaving means mixing related problem types instead of finishing a full block of one type first. It can make practice feel slower, but the variety should help transfer to unfamiliar exam questions.

## Revision Routine

### Best Review Method

Rereading the chapter several times is the most effective way to learn because each pass feels faster and more familiar. Highlighting the key sentences during the final pass should confirm that I know them.

### Weekly Checklist

- Close the slides before answering recall questions.
- Keep short reviews on the calendar.
- Mix related problem sets near the end of the week.
- Reread highlighted sections before the exam.
